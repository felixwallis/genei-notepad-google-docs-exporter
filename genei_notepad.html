Notes for "3.3 - What (If Anything) Is Wrong with Positive Liberty?"

Overview
  • Isaiah Berlin's essay, "Two Concepts of Liberty," defends political philosophy.
  • Berlin cautions "Philosophical concepts nurtured in the stillness of the philosopher's study could destroy a civilization".
To avoid this fate, Berlin thinks, we must commit to the critical analysis of political concepts.
  • Berlin's distinction between negative and positive concepts of liberty has spurred sharp criticism for decades.
  • Most of Berlin's essay is focused on the problems with positive liberty.
  • Many contemporary scholars read Berlin's criticisms as products of his Cold War context and his anti-Communism.
  • The threat was clearly from negative liberty: the "liberal ultra-individualism" that Berlin had dismissed (ibid).
  • Berlin's worries about positive liberty now seemed like the hyperbolic vestiges of reaction against a dead worldview.
  • The implication of this reading is that Berlin's criticisms have been overtaken.
  • But this is too hasty. Even if Berlin's criticisms were the product of his context and his political commitments, it does not follow that they are wrong.
This article is an attempt to give Berlin’s criticisms of positive liberty the critical attention they deserve.
In the first section, McQueen examines two different accounts of positive liberty that Berlin offers in the essay.
McQueen shows that they are not consistent and that each has different implications for the contrast that he draws between negative and positive liberty.
The second section examines the “inner-citadel” worry about positive freedom, the third section examines the “moraliza- tion” worry, and the fourth examines the “tyranny” worry. In each case, McQueen evaluates whether the criticism is a strong one.
McQueen takes a strong criticism ofpositive liberty to be one that:
(1) Clearly attaches to one or more features of a conception of positive liberty.
(2) That defenders of positive liberty do not have the resources to block.
(3) That applies uniquely or with far greater frequency to positive than to negative conceptions of liberty.
McQueen finds that
each of Berlin's criticisms attaches to at least one feature of a conception of positive liberty.
However, none of his worries are speciﬁc to positive conceptions of liberty.
In short, Berlin’s worries may be good concerns about conceptions of liberty in general. But they are not compelling criticisms of positive liberty in particular.

What is positive liberty?

The challenge of assessing Berlin’s worries about positive liberty starts with ﬁguring out what he actually means by the term.
  • Berlin glosses positive liberty as the absence of deliberate external human interference with whatever "I could otherwise do".
  • Negative liberty is about "how many doors are open, how open they are, upon their relative importance in my life".
  • In contrast, positive liberty is about the action itself-the capacity to walk through the open doors. It is, invoking Taylor again, an "exercise-concept". Call this Positive Liberty 1, or PL1.
Berlin draws even more heavily on a second way of understanding positive liberty.
On this view, freedom is self-mastery.
  • Berlin draws even more heavily on a second way of understanding positive liberty. On this view, freedom is self-mastery. To have self-mastery on this view is to act according to the dictates of one's higher self. This is freedom.
One can be rendered both nega- tively and positively unfree through the interference or domination of one’s higher self by external forces.
But one can also be rendered positively unfree by internal interference or domination by one’s lower self.
The views of positive freedom that occupy Berlin have the higher self associated with reason or the agent''s moral nature. The lower self is associated with passions or appetite.
Call this Positive Liberty 2, or PL2.
Berlin's two versions of positive liberty can be summarised as follows:
PL1 focuses on the freedom to pursue and attain one’s ends. This version of positive liberty is compatible with negative liberty, but is more demanding.
PL2 focuses on self-mastery and has several variants depending on the conditions one thinks are necessary for self-mastery. PL2 is sometimes incompatible with negative liberty and not always in the same ways.

The inner-citadel worry

Berlin offers three sets of criticisms of positive liberty that McQueen labels the inner-citadel worry, the moralization worry, and the tyranny worry.
Berlin argues that if we conceive of freedom as self-mastery (PL2), then an agent is free when she acts according to the dictates of her higher self
Her actions may be constrained by internal forces, like the appetites and passions of her lower self.
Her actions may also be con- strained by external forces, like “the laws of nature, or by accidents, or the activities of men, or the effect, often undesigned, of human insti- tutions”
When faced with external constraints, the agent can become free in one of two ways:
She can remove the constraints.
She can revise the dictates of her higher self.
  • PL2 views hold that freedom means acting on the dictates of one's higher self. This is freedom achieved by shedding desires, transforming the self, and retreating to the inner citadel. But is it really freedom?
  • Berlin thinks not. He reasons that
“the doctrine that maintains that what I cannot have I must teach myselfnot to desire, that a desire eliminated, or successfully resisted, is as good as a desire sat- isﬁed, is a sublime, but, it seems to me unmistakable, form ofthe doctrine ofsour grapes: what I cannot be sure of, I cannot truly want”.
  • Many readers share Berlin's intuition that one is not made freer by adapting one's preferences in the face of interference. Do positive conceptions of liberty have the resources to block a retreat to the inner citadel? McQueen argues that it does.
The most promising route requires distinguishing between desire changes that are adaptive and those that are innocuous or positive.
For example, if a student changes their mind about a higher-order desire to go to university as a result
of getting a better understanding of her values or the best means to achieve her ends, then she has not retreated to the inner citadel. She has just updated her desires.
This student would be freer than a student who maintained a higher level desire for a university education despite their home country not allowing it.

Why the inner-citadel worry is misleading

Berlin frames the inner-citadel worry as a criticism of positive views of liberty, and self-mastery views in particular. This is misleading.
consider that any view of freedom has to have a story about what counts as a constraint.
One view
afﬁrmed by
defenders of both positive and negative liberty is that a constraint
is something that frustrates the desires of an agent.
This means that whether or not something counts as a restraint depends on the agent’s desires. An individual can increase their freedom by changing their desires.
Any conception of freedom, whether positive or negative, that tethers restraints to desires in this way risks retreating to the inner citadel.
Berlin’s own conception of negative liberty runs into this
problem.
If I want to increase my freedom, I can simply convince myself that the possibility I am restrained from pursuing is not that important to me.
  • Berlin tries to correct this problem in a later essay. He argues that negative liberty is not "the absence of obstacles to the fulfilment of a man's desires".
Freedom, he now writes, entails
"The absence of obstacles to possible choices and activities—absence of obstructions on roads along which a man can decide to walk."
  • Berlin has brought himself right back to the problem he most wanted to avoid.
  • If my freedom depends upon the "relative importance in my life" of the options before me, then I can make myself freer by convincing myself that certain options are of no importance to me.
This difﬁculty raises deeper questions about the coherence of Berlin’s account of freedom.

The moralisation worry

Berlin is also worried about conceptions offreedom that identify it with other values, such as justice, rationality, virtue, or adherence to the moral law.
Berlin’s frustration at the identiﬁcation of freedom with other values like justice is a product of his value pluralism.
As a political pluralist, he was alive to the dangers of deﬁning freedom in terms of other contestable values or ideals.
Berlin was also concerned that we moralize liberty when we identify it with other values.
If we hold a moralized conception of freedom, our thinking changes.
To illustrate this consider two individuals, Willa and Victor.
Willa pitches a tent in Victor’s backyard without his permission. Victor calls the police in order to remove Willa and her tent from his property.
If “freedom is not freedom to do what is … wrong” and if violating the property rights of others is wrong, then enforcing Willa’s duty to respect Victor’s property rights by forcibly removing her from his yard is not a restriction of her liberty.
Our evaluation of Victor’s and the state’s actions is now more constrained.
We cannot consider the possibility that Willa’s eviction is both a restriction of her liberty and justiﬁed. If this is a possibility worth entertaining, then we should avoid moralized views of liberty.
We can easily see that positive conceptions of liberty, particularly those which see liberty as self-mastery, are moralized in this way.
These conceptions often identify liberty with other values. On many such views, to have self-mastery simply is to act in accordance
with reason or virtue or duty.
As shown by Nozick,
the possibility of moralization extends to negative views of freedom. Nozick claims
“Other people’s actions place limits on one’s available opportunities. Whether this makes one’s resulting action non-voluntary depends upon whether these others had the right to act as they did”.
Some philosophers argue that negative conceptions ofliberty can also be moralized in a more subtle way as when we consider what counts as interference (a constraint on liberty according to the negative view), we
inevitably make assumptions about the domain of human pursuits that are thwarted by these constraints.
As John Christman
argues, “fences only one foot high cannot be counted as a constraint for normal adults, unless, that is, such adults feel obligated to participate in a religious ritual that involves shufﬂing along without lifting their feet and such fences are in the way.”
In specifying what counts as a constraint, we make assumptions about what sorts of human pursuits are normal and morally valuable.
Ultimatley, everyone,
including theorists of negative freedom, share widely held assumptions about which pursuits are more or less important to humans as moral, purposive beings
In practice, then, most theorists ofnegative liberty are not concerned with deliberate human interference with whatever “I could otherwise do”; they are con- cerned with deliberate human interference with whatever valuable and/ or purposive thing “I could otherwise do.”
It is therefore evident that
both positive and negative views of liberty can be moralized.
Thus McQueen, so far has shown that
two of Berlin’s main criticisms of positive conceptions of liberty also apply to negative ones.

The tyranny worry

  • Berlin's most serious worry about positive liberty is that it can easily become tyrannical. His worry is directed at conceptions of positive liberty as self-mastery.
  • Paternalism consists in restricting the liberty of another person for the sake of that person's welfare, interests, or good. The freedom that is restricted in cases of paternalism might be negative liberty, positive liberty, or some other kind of liberty not captured by these labels.
  • The paternalist recognizes "that it is possible, and at times justifiable, to coerce men in the name of some goal" (Berlin). The paternalist coerces others for their sake and not for hers.
While we might engage in ﬁerce debates about the legitimacy of paternalism, we can at least all be clear on what is going on. Liberty is being restricted.
on a positive liberty view, a new possibility presents itself. Berlin elaborates
'I may declare that [those I am coercing] are actually aiming at what in their benighted state they consciously resist, because there exists within them an occult entity—their latent rational will, or their “true” purpose—and that this entity, although it is belied by all that they overtly feel and do and say, is their “real” self, ofwhich the poor empirical self in space and time may know nothing or little; and that this inner spirit is the only selfthat deserves to have its wishes taken into account. Once I take this view, I am in a pos- ition to ignore the actual wishes of men or societies, to bully, oppress, torture them in the name, and on behalf, of their “real” selves, in the secure knowledge that whatever is the true goal ofman … must be iden- tical with his freedom—the free choice of his “true,” albeit often sub- merged and inarticulate, self.'
Berlin describes this move as a “sleight ofhand” and a “perversion”
By some trick, one can become an apologist for
coercion in the name of liberty. This trick is illustrated in three moves:
PL2 claim: For an individual (A) to be free, she must act in accordance with the dictates of her higher self.
Obstruction claim: Something is blocking A from acting in accordance with the dictates of her higher self and, therefore, is blocking her from being free.
Epistemic claim: A second individual (B) knows better than A what A's higher self wants.
Berlin imagines that the epistemic claim is grounded in supreme conﬁdence about which ends must have priority for everyone. If B can see A's higher aims better than A, B is in a position to ignore A's actual wishes, to interfere with her in the name of freeing her from her lower self.
If B is the representative of a larger group or political authority, she can make the further following claim:
Political condition claim: Only in Political Society X can A and others reliably act in accordance with the dictates of their higher selves.
Therefore, the realistion of Political Society X is necessary for A and others to be free.
Berlin argues that an an implication of this reasoning,
any interference, oppression, or torture employed in bringing political society X into being are simply necessary steps in the project of freedom.
This is the “sleight of hand” that transforms the most appalling acts of tyranny into conditions for liberation.

Rejecting Berlin's tyranny worry

One immediate objection to Berlin’s tyranny worry is a logical one.
Positive liberty does not lead directly or necessarily to coercion.
If what self-mastery requires is that A’s higher self actively brings her lower self to heel (Strong Self-Mastery, PL2), then B simply cannot interfere with A in the name of liberty. Hence,
one way to prevent Berlin’s worry from getting off the ground is to insist upon the strongest and most active understanding of self-mastery.
Second, we can accept that freedom requires self-mastery and that something is blocking A from achieving this, whilst rejecting the claim that B is in any better position to know what A wants than he is. In this case, B's own views about ends need not be a reliable guide to what A truly wants.
Humans are fallible. We should expect this fallibility to increase when trying to discern the ends of others. And if we have the liberal intuition that power tends to corrupt, then we might think that those with political power are likely to be especially fallible.
If B is a political agent, then she might be peculiarly prone to mis- taking A’s ends, particularly when they do not coincide with the ends of the regime.
Clearly there is no
logical necessity about the perversion of positive liberty.
Yet Berlin never claims that there is.
Rather, Berlin’s claim is that such a perversion of positive freedom is both possible and likely.
In order for tyranny to be pressed into the service ofliberation, Berlin thinks a conception of freedom needs to posit some notion of a divided self.
Liberty would then consist in the absence of deliberate human interference with this ideal or higher self.
Berlin argues, with good reason, that negative conceptions rarely make this move, and that PL2 views almost invariably do. He attributes this distortion
to some features of PL2 views that he thinks make them especially liable to this fate.
he also thinks that certain background conditions make this perversion more likely
Berlin clearly has the Soviet Union, its satellites, and other explicitly Marxist or Communist regimes in mind.
Yet Berlin does not make an empirical case to support this concept. Instead, he presents an account of the intellectual history of positive freedom that purports to track its perversion over time, but which numerous scholars have demonstrated is often distorted, incomplete and misleading in its interpretation of particular thinkers.
Even if the empirical case can be made, defenders ofpositive freedom can protect themselves against the possibility of perversion by insisting upon the strongest and most active understanding of self-mastery.
If we interpret self-mastery as requiring that the agent’s higher self actively brings his lower self “to heel”
then neither
the regime nor its representatives can coherently interfere with the agent in the name of liberty.

Conclusion

McQueen has examined each
of Berlin’s worries about positive liberty, showing that while
all of Berlin’s concerns plausibly attach to features of positive liberty views, they all apply to at least some conceptions of negative liberty as well.
defen- ders of positive conceptions of liberty have the resources to block the inner-citadel and tyranny worries. They may be good worries about con- ceptions of liberty; they are just not good criticisms of positive liberty according to the standards established at the outset.
Berlin
memorably
expresses what is so disturbing about the jus- tiﬁcation of force, oppression, and torture as tools of liberation.
But wrongs perpetrated in the name of freedom, whether positive or negative, are also complicated ones.
To lay these wrongs at the feet of positive liberty is to give it both too much power and too much responsibility.
