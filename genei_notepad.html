Overview

Freedom as noninterference

In debates about justice and the economy, those
classified as ‘right-wing’ present themselves as defenders of economic freedom understood as the defense of lightly regulated markets and robust rights of property and contract.
those
broadly classified as ‘left-wing’ present themselves as restricting economic freedom in the name of other values, like social justice or solidarity.
these positions rest on an inconsistent and vague use of the language of freedom.
The right-wing position illegitimately presents itself as the only ‘pro-freedom’ one while, on the other side, the left-wing view need not be understood as limiting free- dom for the sake of other values.
If we take the common understanding of freedom seri- ously, conceptual possibilities open up for appreciating an alternative position, committed to economic freedom, but with a different set of institutional arrangements in mind.
The standard definition of freedom Gourevitch uses
is freedom as noninterference.
In the main- stream discussion of economic freedom, there are at least two conceptual problems with the use of this concept:
inconsistency in the use of the term freedom
indeterminacy with respect to relevant freedoms scholars wish to defend.
Inconsistency in the use ofthe term freedom has mistakenly made the left-wing critique of liberal capitalism appear to be a concern with inequality in means to enjoy freedom, not with freedom itself
Inde- terminacy about what we should be free to do makes the left-wing position mistakenly appear to be about positive liberty, or security, or happiness.
These conceptual mistakes lead to misrepresentations of possible positions one might take on economic freedom.
They give the right-wingers an unjustified rhetorical advantage by claiming to defend economic freedom
Howeverm, when the left argues that it defends real economic freedom, it is (often) not arguing for more ‘means to enjoy’ freedom.
Rather, it is arguing for more freedoms for most people, or for different freedoms, or both. It is able to make that claim using a standard definition of freedom as noninterference.

Freedom as noninterference

Recent debates about the ‘economic liberties’ have assumed a
definition offree- dom as noninterference.
For
neoclassical liberals, a just social order guarantees ‘a thick conception ofeconomic liberty’ because it gives all citizens basic rights securing them from interference in their attempts to acquire property, save money, make labor contracts, and take economic risks
Opponents of this classical liberal view have tended to assume a similar definition of freedom.
though
authors do not always make explicit that they are adopting the
freedom as noninterference view,3 it is clear from their use of language -
‘unqualified rights of private property in productive resources and other economic liberties’ should not be given ‘nearly equal significance with other basic liberties’ (Samuel Freeman) -
that this is the concept they rely on.
current scholars are committed to the noninterference view from the fact that they never adopt the moralized conception offreedom, associated with
Locke and Nozick.
On that view, we are free so long as we act consistently with the moral law and/or if a person has a moral right to restrict another person’s choices, then those restrictions are not freedom compromising.
If current debates rest on the noninterference view offreedom, scholars are not always careful about their use of the term.
on this definition, I am free to do something when nobody interferes with my doing that thing.
Normally, the relevant interference is human and that interference is lawful.
This
restriction on the definition offreedom to refer only to human interference is not valid in all conversations about freedom.
in
discussions about eco- nomic liberties, it is reasonable to distinguish between freedom and ability in terms of human versus natural obstacles because in that discourse the concern is first and fore- most with social and political freedoms – obstacles placed or removed by human design.
However, Gourevitch is able to illustrate his argument
without having to expand or redefine the relevant understanding of interference as it relates to
legally permitted interference
According to Gourevitch,
I have a liberty to do something when there is no legal obstacle to my perfor- mance of that action.
Such a
‘liability to legal interference’ definition
is widely accepted and captures the common intuition that we are not free to do what the law prohibits even ifwe are able to do that prohibited action.

Freedom as noninterference

inconsistency in the application of
the standard view of liberty
leads to misleading statements about poverty and markets.
inconsistencies
can be traced back to
figures
like Rawls and Berlin
For instance, Rawls argues that
the distinction between liberty and the worth of liberty is
merely a definition and settles no substantive question
Gourevitch contests this view.
If having a liberty and enjoying the fair value of a liberty get different substantive, moral treatment
then successfully distinguishing having a liberty from the worth of that liberty will be one way of settling substantive questions.
It transforms the ‘radical democrat and socialist’ argument into a concern with the worth ofliberty not with the possession of liberty
Gourevitch focuses
on Rawls’ way of drawing the distinction because it continues to be a touchstone and is explicitly presented as a response to a kind of left-wing challenge.
Using MacCallum’s definition of freedom as a 'triadic realtion'
to motivate the distinction between a person hav- ing a liberty and enjoying the worth of that liberty requires a specification of which things ‘count’ as obstacles and a specification of what beings and doings they obstruct.
Rawls keeps with the standard ‘noninterference’ view that the relevant obstacles are legal ones
Thus the left-wing argument may energe that
liberals care only about legal obsta- cles but lefties care about economic obstacles to doing things.
However, in the following Gourevitch
turn to the underlying conceptual inconsis- tency driving this belief, focusing his critique on the idea that poverty affects only the worth ofa liberty.
Such inconsistency lies in believing poverty is about inability rather than liability to legal interference.
poverty is not inability at all.
It is a condition of increased liability to legal interfer- ence, or unfreedom.
Failing to appreciate this fact, various thinkers have continued to misunderstand the left-wing objection.

Freedom reexamined: Poverty and the redistribution of freedoms

on the standard view freedom is distinguished from ability because ability refers to the capacity to overcome physical and mental obstacles to doing things, not human, legal ones.
On this prevailing view
ill- ness, poverty and ignorance are all disabilities because there is a lack of physical and mental means to do things.
Poverty, however, is not inability.
the essence ofpoverty is not a deprivation ofability but offreedom
That is because, without money, a person faces legal interference with the actions he or she is otherwise able to
perform.
As G. A. Cohen puts it, when it comes to most goods, ‘giving money is both necessary for getting them, and, indeed, sufficient for getting them, if they are on sale. If you attempt access to them in the absence ofmoney, then you will be prey to interfer- ence’
a major reason poverty can cause disability is because a per- son is unfree to remedy that disability.
poverty makes one liable to interference and
restricts one’s liberty with respect to property, in ways that having money does not.
A poor individual looking for shelter is unable to remove the legal interference that stops them from staying in someone's unused property. In contrast, a rich person in the same scenario is able to remove the legal interference to their shelter by purchasing a house.
Generalising this concept, it is evident that the poor face more legal interference with respect to their actions than the nonpoor.
In other words, money acts as a
kind of state- issued ticket but without all the possible uses of it written down.
in virtue of having fewer of those tickets, or less money, an individual
faces many more non-removable legal obstacles than a person with more money.
Since money is just about the only way to remove legal obstacles to goods, a pooere person's
freedom is more restricted than someone with more money. That unfreedom is a general feature of his poverty.
Money is ‘social in its very essence’ because the special role of money is to remove legal not natural obstacles.
An implication of this argument, first outlined by G. A. Cohen is that
there might be other social conventions, besides money, that could eliminate legal obsta- cles.
For example, there are ways, besides money, to eliminate legal obstacles to accessing goods, such as philanthropy.
Thus, money
is just one possible social convention by which the legally sanctioned power of interference is distributed.
The more that access to goods is depen- dent upon them being offered on the market for money, the more that money serves its social function of removing legal obstacles and redistributing freedoms.
More money is
more freedom; lack of money is less freedom.
It is therefore incon- sistent for theorists to argue as they have: that liberty is noninterference, that interference is a matter of liability to legal obstacles, but that poverty is disability not unfreedom.
Lack of money is nothing but increased liability to interference in the access to goods. It is therefore restriction of liberty.
Hence,
at least one part ofthe liberal response to left-wing criticisms is not valid.
The ‘rad- ical democrats and socialists’
are not worried about the worth but about the extent of liberties.
Once we reject the view that poverty is disability, we cannot say that it merely affects the worth of liberties.

Freedom regained

The left-wing view argues for various redistributive schemes for the sake of redistributing liberties and for increasing the liberties that the poor have.

Taxing to increase freedom

Consider a situation of socialised provision of housing.
Imagine the state made an apartment available to anyone unable to afford rent, simply upon request.
Nobody
would have the legal permission to prevent an individual from gaining access to housing.
say that the state pays for providing housing
by taxing the wealthy
On the standard liberal view
‘taxing and
spending’ is seen as the restriction of a liberty in the form of liberty of property.
But on the left-wing view
Coer- cive taxation restricts the wealthy person’s liberty; the supply of legal access to housing increases the poor person’s.
There are two
implications of this view of money and freedom:
in a capitalist society, income transfers are less freedom-restricting ways of providing access to goods than in-kind transfers.
For instance, a person with a monthly stipend from the state can remove interference with his or her access to housing across a wide range of rental housing options, but a person with a claim on in-kind state-provided housing can only remove interference with access to the narrower range of public housing options.
Income transfers are most freedom increasing when money is the dominant convention for altering powers of interference with access to goods and services.

Abolishing markets to increase freedom

The relationship Gourevitch highlights between money and markets point to a second implication of
ofthe analysis offreedom and distribution.
Ifmoney only fully serves its role in removing legal obstacles when most goods have been commodified, then an argument against the use of markets can be one about preferring one mechanism for distributing freedoms over another.
an argu- ment against using markets can be an argument for restricting freedom in the name of freedom.
there is a freedom-related version of the argument
for abolishing markets
we can see arguments for redis- tribution, and even abolishing markets, as about redistributing and expanding, not just restricting, freedom.
A radical example of the argument that abolishing a market is a way ofproviding, not just restricting, a liberty, is the market for productive assets.
when means of production are privately owned most people are unfree to own any
productive assets.
private ownership means that the owners may exclude workers from the use of those productive assets and only give them access by giving them wages and benefits in exchange for
labor.
Any unemployed person who wants a job faces legal inter- ference ifhe or she attempts to gain access to means ofproduction without the owners’ permission.
We could imagine a society in which, as part of the state ownership of the means of production, each person has a right to employment in any state-owned enterprise, and ifno existing manager ofa state-owned enterprise will accept any given person as an employee, then that person would have a legal claim against the state to supply him or her with some form of employment
In this example, one liberty – private ownership of productive assets – has been restricted, but another liberty – freedom from legal obstacles to having some job or another – has been increased.
The abolition of a market in productive assets is, on this view, necessary for everyone to possess a liberty with respect to those means of production.
abolishing a market in productive assets, which involves a deep restric- tion on an economic liberty, would expand the number of people with the liberty to control productive assets.

Freedom and institutional evaluation

money can only serve as an instrument offreedom against certain background conditions: so long as there are markets where goods and services are available for sale, money can eliminate legal barriers to accessing those commodities.
More money means more freedom only on condition that there are already well-developed markets in goods and services.
ifmoney increases freedom only on that condition, then it cannot also be used as an argument for why that condition of lightly regulated markets should exist in the first place.
That is a tautology in which a explains b and b explains a: ifthere are markets then money is freedom, money is freedom therefore we need markets.
the attempt to compare and evaluate entire economic sys- tems from the standpoint of the degree to which they are freedom respecting cannot rest merely on whether one generates more or less money value or even just on how it dis- tributes that money.
That confuses means with ends.
Money is but one means of avoiding legal interference and that only under certain institutional conditions.
Under other institutional conditions, there might be a much more limited role for money, but there might be other instruments for removing and distributing legal barriers to action.

Freedom lost again: The indeterminacy of 'thick' and 'thin' economic liberties

liberty involves a triadic relation between (a) agents, (b) obstacles and (c) doings/beings.
there is a distinct question about which kinds ofeconomic doings/beings the law should or should not be interfering with.
contemporary literature states the relevant doings and beings, like ‘lib- erty to own productive assets’ or ‘liberty ofworking’, in far too vague or general a way to know which basic liberty is
being defended.
This indeterminacy has mud- died the waters ofmoral contention by creating the false impression that the only dis- agreement is over whether one is dedicated to a ‘thick’ or ‘thin’ conception of basic economic liberties or over a particular conception of the person who bears and enjoys these liberties.
libertarians and classical liberals claim to defend
‘thick’ conceptions of economic liberty against the ‘thin’ conceptions of high liberals.
All participants in the debate
agree that the classical liberals defend everyone’s freedom to engage in a wider range of eco- nomic activities than high liberals do.
this is a strange description of theoretical possibilities
because the content of the economic liberties is significantly underspecified.
what is supposed to make the classical liberal view ‘thick’ is that it also defends a basic liberty to ownership of productive assets, while high liberals only defend a basic liberty to own personal property
But a liberty to own productive assets
can refer to a number of
incompatible eco- nomic activities.
For instance, the
liberty to own productive assets could be interpreted to mean that everyone should enjoy equal rights to try to acquire and keep productive assets.
This liberty guarantees that each person has a formal possibility to own
productive property.
This is the currently dominant interpretation of that liberty among nearly all authors
this account of the relevant liberty is consistent with a society in which it would be impos- sible for everyone to actually own productive assets.
on this interpreta- tion
everyone could
have this liberty to own while living in a society in which most people face unremovable legal obstacles to owning any productive assets.
They could be free to try to acquire productive assets, without actually being in a position to acquire any on the market – say because the only jobs available to some of them pay too little for them to be able to save enough to invest or buy shares in productive assets.
Since the market is the only way to acquire nearly all productive assets in this private-property regime, these people are unfree to own private property.
the liberty to own productive assets is therefore consistent with a society in which most people do not have a different liberty
They do not have that liberty to own actual assets because they face a legal regime organized around a different interpretation of the same liberty to own productive assets.
Yet the liberty to own productive assets could also refer to a different kind of eco- nomic activity that we should be free to do.
it could mean everyone is free from legal obstacles to possessing some share ofproductive assets.
A society that secured that particular liberty would have to restrict
formal rights to acquire property in productive assets since
it would have to be illegal for someone or some group to acquire so much property that is not enough would be left for everyone else to possess their relevant share
on this interpretation of the liberty to own productive assets, these restrictions would not be the narrowing or ‘thinning’ ofeconomic liberties in the way classical liberals say
It would be a restriction of the freedom to buy and sell productive assets in order to ensure everyone enjoys the liberty to own some actual productive assets.
Thus,
liberty to own productive assets can mean at least two things depending on what it is we should be free from legal obstacles to doing.
Either is a reasonable interpretation of the same, undetermined liberty to own productive assets.
But it is not clear what it would mean to say that one view is a ‘thicker’ or ‘thinner’ conception.
Each conception makes a liberty available on condition that other related liberties may legitimately and are very likely to be restricted.

Moral disagreement about persons or conceptual confusion about liberties?

left-Rawlsian defenders of‘workplace democracy’ understand
‘powers of control over productive resources’
to include certain legal rights of control over the workplace
Why call this a commitment to a narrower set of economic liberties, rather than to a different set – potentially a ‘thicker’ set of lib- erties?
high liberals like Freeman and Rawls don’t make such a move
because the ‘thick’ versus ‘thin’ distinction is supposed to describe
basic liberties,
and there are
few liberties that high liberals are willing to make basic.
high liberals
say that the liberty to own productive assets
is not a basic liberty because they do not think it is necessary for the ade- quate development of the two moral powers
Gourevitch argues that high liberals
have too quickly accepted the domi- nant assumption that the liberty to own productive assets can only mean something like the liberty to acquire whatever you manage to acquire, even if in practice that means acquiring nothing.
this is to accept the def- inition of an economic liberty that has,
been stated in terms
too indeter- minate for us to know what it is that we are supposed to be free to do.
This conceptual indeterminacy makes it difficult to resolve or even address some of the moral disagree- ments that arise regarding the economic liberties
a substantial amount ofthis dispute is
about a failure to state clearly enough what a person should be free to do and be.
For instance, having
security, independence, and opportunity to express one’s identity are
necessary conditions for developing the capacities ofself-authorship
Such arguments that attempt to
connect some argument about security, independence or identity expression to the development of powers of self-authorship is supposed to justify basic liberties of ownership.
Yet that moral claim is hampered as even if
we knew with much greater detail what ‘security’ ‘indepen- dence’ and ‘identity expression’ are, we do not know which actual liberties ofownership they are supposed to ground.
the relevant liberty has been defined in too vague and indeterminate a way for us to know how it is connected to its foundations in the development ofmoral powers.
the indeterminacy means we are left with no way of dealing with contradictions between
having versus possibly acquiring property.
So long as the economic liberties are stated at a level so indeterminate about what one ought to be free to do or be, we cannot evaluate any claims about the causal connection between the development of human capacities and the pos- session of these liberties.

Freedom regained, again

Gourevitch has shown that
the indeterminate specification of the basic economic liberties means that the high liberal view could be represented as defending a different and poten- tially ‘thick’ set of economic liberties, and that, regardless of whether we characterize the positions as ‘thick’ and ‘thin’, their disagreements are based not just on moral differ- ences about ideals of the person but also on conceptual confusion.
Such confusion leads to a misunderstanding
of the kind of challenge that a left-wing view regarding economic liberties poses for classical and high liberals.
On the left-wing view, the classical liberal version ofthe liberty to own productive assets formally permits
a monopolization ofall ofsociety’s productive assets by
a minority of its members
this interpretation of the liberty to own productive assets is presented as the only conceptual possibility when the left-wing view is that this liberty should be understood as a legal claim that every member of soci- ety has to actually own and control some share of productive assets.
Such an interpretation obscures the left-wing view
because very important economic liberties are stated at such a high level of generality that they are seriously indeterminate, yet they are assumed to have one interpretation.
So when John Tomasi argues that his interpretation of classical liberalism ‘affirms a general right of economic liberty’ and believes this to be a distinguishing feature of classical liberal- ism, he makes an error, since the left-wing view can also be understood as affirming a ‘general right of economic liberty’
The error is
compounded when Tomasi
believes this economic liberty is uniquely identified with certain formal rights ofproperty and contract as if this were the only coherent inter- pretation of the liberty ‘of acquiring and holding property’
Others are just as guilty of the error and thus of criticizing straw man leftism.

Freedom as noninterference

Gourevitch has aimed to show that
the standard view of freedom as noninterference
is inconsistently and indeterminately used.
As such, contemporary debates illegitimately cast left-wing arguments as concerned exclu- sively with the ‘value’ of basic liberties.
An important implication of this analysis has ben to observe the tautology involved in arguing that
money is freedom when there are markets and that, therefore, markets are freedom increasing because they pro- duce more goods available for sale.
A further contribution has been to show the
compatibility of socialism with negative liberty.
a socialist argument for redistribution of money and goods, and even for abolishing markets, is logically consistent with and internally coher- ent, given the definition of liberty as noninterference.
once the specific liberties are analyzed and conceptual ambiguities removed, we can see both that the left-wing position is consistent with a standard definition of liberty and that
conceptual confusion is being misrepresented as moral disagreement.
Such clarification opens up
further areas of investigation by showing how existing positions misinterpret or obscure alternative possibilities.
