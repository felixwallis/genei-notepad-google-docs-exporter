Notes for "7.3 - What Do Bureaucrats Like Doing? Bureaucratic Preferences in Response to Institutional Reform"

Overview
Research suggests that members of public agencies use administrative reform to advance their policy goals
Gains and John seek to explain
how senior bureaucrats realize their administrative preferences when the design of their bureau is altered, thereby changing the roles of politicians and bureaucrats.
This article inves- tigates the relationship between administrative reform and how bureaucrats think about their administrative roles and behav- iors
literature on institutional design oper- ates with simple
accounts of the
preferences of bureaucrats, assuming that officials respond to maintain their autonomy in the face of changes in the design of their bureaus.
There is an assumption that public servants, without a carefully designed incentive structure, will act in their short-term self-interest and may work against the public welfare in seeking to inflate the budgets of their bureaus.
This basic budget- maximizing thesis has been modified to argue that bureaucrats seek to bureau-shape in order to achieve their preferred working environment (Dunleavy 1991).
unleavy’s premise is that bureaucrats prefer a congenial policy advice role close to core politi- cal decision makers.
Drawing on survey data of 471 bureaucrats in 40 local government areas in England, Gains and John
show support for a bureau-shaping account but also show
variation in bureaucratic preferences.
They argue
that institutional redesign and reform should take into account the differences in bureaucratic preferences.

Bureaucratic behaviour and institutional reform

Gains and John seek to understand
what kinds of work bureaucrats prefer to do and the implications of these preferences for the design of their bureaus.
Bureaucrats carry out a variety of tasks
that include advising, mak- ing decisions, dealing with external stakehold- ers, and getting results.
institu- tions vary in the extent to which they stress one job
and not another.
Bureaucrats
reveal different prefer- ences about what kinds of work they like to do.
Their preference for certain kinds of job tasks may have implications for how bureaucrats respond to institutional reform.
This is especially so when the design of the bureau is altered, thereby changing the roles of politicians and bureaucrats and providing opportunities for bureaucrats to shape their working environments.
public choice literature argues
that public servants will act in their short-term self-interest and may use their discretion to work against the public welfare by seeking to inflate the budgets of their bureaus.
However, the budget-maiximising premise has been empirically challenged, most prominantly by Dunleavy (1991) who
pointed out the collective action problems faced by bureaucrats seeking to maximize budgets and the way in which budget-maximizing strategies would vary according to the rank of the official, agency, and budget type.
The bureau-shaping model developed by Dunleavy
aimed to calibrate
the preferences of bureaucrats for a particular institutional design.
Dunleavy assumes
senior bureaucrats have nonpecuniary preferences for intellectually stimulating but low-cost policy advice work with little or no management responsi- bilities.
Therefore, senior bureaucrats only seek to expand the size of their core budgets
in order to shape their working environment to achieve these prefer- ences.
They do not want to head up heavily staffed, large budget but routine, conflictual and low status agencies.
Despite Dunleavy's work, there
remains a gap in our knowledge about what kinds of work bureaucrats like and whether those preferences are distributed across the officer corps or whether the role socializes bureaucrats.
Those at the
core of the organization may operate in a more political and policy- making world, with close contacts with politicians, whereas other senior bureaucrats may head up large service-based bureaucracies
and may have more of an interest in delivery.
Gains and John seek
to investigate these assump- tions by examining expressed task prefer- ences and how these relate to time spent on reported job tasks and bureaucratic attitudes toward reform.

Institutional reform in English local govnerment and the revealing of bureaucratic preferences

English local government is a useful case for conducting
study of political-bureaucratic relationships.
local govern- ment officials in the United Kingdom are appointed on merit, they are politically neutral, and they are permanent.
English local government is aso of interest to the study of bureaucracy because the
system of political leadership has been reformed by the 1997 Labour government's 2000 Local Government Act
which aimed to create stronger leadership by a small group of elected politicians who would be held accountable by strong overview and scrutiny committees composed of a wider group of representatives.
Since 2002, when the act was implemented, formal power has become concentrated in a small group of elected executive councilors.
the reform has led to a strengthening of the political executive, with a clearer focus on policy making and enhancement of the professional executive politician
However, the option to delegate decision-making authority over some matters to individual senior bureaucrats remained follow- ing the legislation.
Decisions that are properly delegated to bureau- crats (who are not politically appointed) are contained in a scheme of delegations and include many decisions in the administrative and management sphere of both the strategic core and functional arms of local authorities.
local officials
are highly unlikely to be able to engage in budget maximization.
However, they do have
“considerable leeway when it comes to internal re- organization and the assignment of tasks”.
Thus, the labour reform
provides the ideal test bed to explore bureaucratic preferences in the face of institutional reform and of the possibility for bureau-shaping activity.

Methods and data

To determine the preferred working tasks of senior bureaucrats, Gains and John draw
on a survey of 471 top-rank senior offices in 40 local governments in England following the introduction of constitutional reform to introduce a separation of powers.
The paper presents descriptive statistics on a series of questions seeking to find out what kinds of work bureaucrats prefer, followed by regres- sion models to see whether these preferences are associated with the work hours spent on specific tasks and satisfaction with the reforms.
In the descriptive statistics of their survey, Gains and John are interested in the varaition in responses that reveal differences in attitudes between bureaucrats.
Gains and John find that bureaucrats
ike the classic bureaucratic aspects of “designing policy” and “taking decisions properly delegated” to them—very large majorities supported these positions.
Turning to the Dunleavy bureau-shaping tasks, there are some difference in responses.
It turns out that bureaucrats do not like the task of “writing policy papers’—they want to be out and about rather than cloistered in offices
But 27 percent, strongly agreed that they like to work in the policy circles suggested in the Dunleavy model, with a further 21 percent strongly prefer- ring to work with politicians. This suggests that a
minority of bureau- crats have this policy advice orientation.
Gains and John find
less preference for implementation and managerial tasks (15 percent) and “nuts and bolts” (12 percent) amongst bureaucrats, and a very low number (5.4 percent) who like direct contact with the public.
Thus, there is
truth to Dunleavy’s
assertion
about the importance of certain kinds of activities that conform to a desired bureau.
Although there is a dislike of writing policy papers,
there is even less preference for implementation and a preference for working in policy circles close their political principals.
Gains and John
seek to ascertain whether bureaucrats select into different types of roles.
To explaore the differentiation of these roles, they investigate
hether the various kinds of factors covary together.
In doing so they find
some support for the idea that there are different kinds of bureaucrats who like to work on different things, with classic policy makers or bureau shapers and implementers as the main division that emerges.
Gains and John's findings
findings show that the preferences of bureaucrats are linked to the jobs they have—which career they have selected into—and/or reflect their experience of a particular job over time.
Thus, the next question Gains and John ask is whether
these bureaucratic preferences correspond to bureau- crats’ desired working environments.
They do this by creating a model for the number of hours spent on certain kinds of activities.
There are intersted to see if bureaucrats are able to
create the kind of environment they want, over and above what is created for them.
An OLS regression shows that
not all of the preferences are associated with the number of hours spent on each kind of task.
This is not
entirely surprising because of the way in which these tasks are predetermined rather than preferred.
Gains and John find that
a preference for working with the leader and portfolio holders is important
Bureaucrats with more of these kinds of hours do not like to make delegated decisions, nor do they like getting the nuts and bolts right.
The authors also find
a preference for working with the leader or mayor and portfolio holders to be significant.
In anohter model, Gains and John
find that those who like to write policy docu- ments do more of this activity, which indicates some bureau-shaping attitudes associated with behavior.
Working with political princi- pals and being in the corporate team are also significant positive predictors.
A final regression model predicts hours that
bureau-shaping bureaucrats should not like, in this case time spent on line management activities.
The model shows that this activity is predicted positively by wanting direct dealings with the public and being a service head, and nega- tively by wanting to work with a small circle of decisions makers and being in the corporate team,
consistent with the bureau-shaping model.
Thus, Gains and John's models
show support for the idea that the different dimensions of bureaucratic attitudes are linked to the varying amounts of time that respondents spend on particular tasks,
Bureaucrats who value the autonomy of the policy-making core seem to be located in a bureau that is designed to achieve their desired goal.

Bureaucrats' attitudes towards reform

Gains and John investigate
bureaucratic attitudes toward reform.
This is an important test of the importance of bureaucratic attitudes because the reforms allow bureaucrats to reveal their preferences for particular kinds of bureaus where they can alter their work pattern in response to changes in the work conditions.
Such thinking is core
to Dunleavy’s formulation.
From the perspective of the bureau-shaping model, reform will be supported if it can deliver increased engagement by senior managers in policy matters by bringing them closer to the center of power and taking away tedious and mundane activities, such as committee work.
Managers would be expected to be strongly in favor of a reform that fosters a closer relationship with the political execu- tive, which is able to get on with decision making.
Gains and John posed a quesiton evaluating whether bureaucrats liked the Labour government's 2000 Local Government Act.
The authors hypothesised that
favorability toward the legislation would depend on the extent in which bureaucrats can shape their bureaus in their own interests.
Gains and John find that bureaucrats
like the act
much less in coalition authorities, which reflects the difficulty of working with more than one principal and the loss of direction and weakening of performance that coalition government brings.
The authors find that wanting to work with the leader or portfolio holder positively predicted liking the act, which is an important element of the bureau-shaping argument.

Conclusion

Gains and John argue that
there has not been enough attention given to researching differences in senior bureaucratic preferences for different job tasks.
at the heart of the bureaucratic role is variation in the different tasks that the job involves, a variation to which few public choice accounts of bureaucratic preferences pay much attention.
these tasks are important for bureaucrats because they define their activities and make a difference in how they evaluate their jobs.
there is a link between attitudes and actions, reflecting the degree to which bureaucrats are free to shape their bureaus to gain a desired balance of work tasks.
ctions become important when political principals seek to reform their bureaus, which allows bureaucrats to make further decisions to follow activities they prefer.
Such actions can have large consequences for how bureaus work and whether reformers get the
policy-making machine they desire or whether bureaucracies work in the same way as before.
Dunleavy’s Bureaucracy, Democracy and the State argues
that bureaucrats will try to shirk management work and crave a more congenial environment close to policy makers, based on their expectations of a policy advice role rather than a role as the manager of a large organization.
Gains and John suggest an alternative argument that
bureaucrats vary in their task preferences, such that some may be bureau shapers, others may be managers.
bureaucrats vary in the extent to which they like certain kinds of work, which will affect the ways in which they or- ganize their bureaus and the extent to which they wish to alter their work balance.
Such considerations will, in turn, affect how they respond to their bureaus in the face of bureaucratic reform.
The authors are unsuprised that the find those in
the policy-making core hold preferences for working on policy, close to policy makers, taking delegated decisions, and working with partners.
service
heads who carry
policies show preferences for implementa- tion tasks.
Gains and John thus show
preferences are associated with the number of hours spent on different tasks.
Their other conclusion is that
when political prin- cipals redesign the executive, bureaucrats support the reform to the extent to which they want to work with those political principals.
Overall, Gains and John's data offers
persuasive evidence of the need for a more nuanced understanding of bureaucratic preferences in line with the bureau-shaping model, and they provide an indication of how these preferences affect support for institutional reform.
For political principals undertaking New Public Management-inspired institutional reforms, an awareness of variation in the preferences of their key officials will be essential in designing the appropriate level of incentives and discretion afforded to bureaucratic agents.
