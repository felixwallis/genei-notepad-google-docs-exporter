Notes for "5.2 - Beyond non-domination"

Overview
Racial inequality still haunts the United States.
Systemic inequalities
reflect and regenerate the limited life- prospects and thwarted agency of the marginalized, and hence they stand in the way of freedom
for many citizens of liberal
democracies
To address this deficit of freedom effectively, we need a clear understanding of the ways that agency and inequality interact, subtly and informally, to undercut freedom.
Understanding freedom as non-domination is a valuable tool for remediating the problem of systematic inequality
Whilst Pettit's theory of non-domination neglects
some of the subtle, unintentional dynamics ofagency and inequality that threaten freedom, it provides a starting point for
a non-sovereign account of human agency that makes plain the deep effects of inequality and points the way to a freedom that goes beyond non-domination.

Domination and its limits

Pettit's republicanism promotes a view of freedom that makes the concept of domination central.
In contrast to negative liberty that views freedom as the mere absence of interference, Pettit's work aims to recover and reconstitute the republican tradition of freedom, showing why it is best conceived in terms of non-domination and how it can be achieved through contemporary liberal-democratic institutions.

Critiques of Pettit's work

Pettit's divide between republicansim and liberlism

Critiques challenge Pettit's divide between republicanism and liberlism.
the conceptual connection between freedom and the rule of law is foundational to what most people call liberalism.
the whole point of the rule of law is that
it protects individuals from living in the perpetual state of vulnerability to arbitrary power that Pettit calls domination.
Hence,
the concerns of republicanism and liberalism are not as far apart as Pettit implies.
Pettit also provides an account of the history of political thought that
dichotomizes the liberal and republican perspectives in ways that are at odds with the actual views of key thinkers in the liberal tradition
From Locke to Kant to J. S. Mill
central tenets of what Pettit calls republicanism are clearly present in the text alongside liberal concerns about interference.
Thus Pettit goes somewhat ‘astray’, as Charles Larmore has said, in the opposition he sets up between the republican conception of freedom and the modern liberal tradition.

Domination as the primary threat to freedom

Scholars critique Pettit's narrowness in casting domination as the primary threat to freedom.
Pettis defined combinations as characterising any relationship in which one person has
‘(1) the capacity to interfere; (2) on an arbitrary basis; (3) in certain choices that the other is in a position to make’.
Crucially, the capacity to interfere need not be actually exercised in order for a condition ofdomination to exist.
Equally important, the requirement that interference must be ‘arbitrary’ if it is to count as domination means that the non-arbitrary constraints on individual choice imposed by legitimate laws do not entail domination but are consistent with liberty.
These features of Pettit’s account distinguish it from the theory of freedom as non-interference.
Krause argues that it is a mistake to
think that domination is the primary obstacle to liberty.
there are other threats as well, some of which are more pressing in contemporary liberal-democratic contexts than domination.
There is no doubt that domination, as convinced by the master-slave dynamic,
undercuts freedom.
Yet the relation- ship ofmastery is often missing from contexts that nevertheless constrain the exercise of individual agency and hinder freedom.
For example, think about a young
black man living in the inner city who is poor, unemployed and ill-educated.
His prospects in life are severely limited, constrained by systematic forces and a cultural background of beliefs and biases that undermines his exercise of agency. Yet he is not ‘under the thumb’ or ‘at the beck and call’ of anyone in particular, and it would be wrong to call him the instrument of some specific person’s will
So although he is not the master of his destiny, neither is he mastered by anyone else which would suggest to Pettit that he is still free.
Krause argues that we
need to be able to recognize and respond to instances of unfreedom such as these, even though they do not fit easily into republicanism’s slavery paradigm or manifest its logic of domination.
The limits of domination in this respect are exacerbated by Pettit’s
insistence that domination must be intentional.
this way of thinking wrongly assumes that obstacles to individual action are either the products of natural (i.e. non-human) forces or result from intentional decisions by discrete human beings (the things we ‘try to do’ to one another).
In reality, many of the things that obstruct our freedom elude both cate- gories.
With racism and sexism for example,
people enact discriminatory norms without intending to do so or even being aware when they do.

Domination as a matter of 'common knowledge'

According to Pettit, domination is a matter of 'common knowledge' in the sense that
‘domination is generally going to involve the awareness of control on the part of the powerful, the awareness of vulner- ability on the part of the powerless, and the mutual awareness – indeed, the common awareness among all the parties to the relationship – of this consciousness on each side’.
This awareness of control cannot be made compatible with an agent whose domination is unintentional so the ‘common knowledge’ character of domination focuses on its intentionality.
To the extent that environmental obstacles are unintentional, they are no part ofdomina- tion
And domination, according to Pettit, is ‘the one and only yardstick by which to judge the social and political constitution ofa community’.
The danger ofexcluding impersonal social factors from the category of domination – where domination is ‘the supreme political value’ and the ‘yardstick of our institutions’ – is that we will fail to recognize the serious threat they pose to freedom.
If we treat non-domination as the sole yardstick of our social and political institutions,
we may be blind to a wide range ofvery powerful threats to freedom.
The cultural back- ground that sustains unintentional racism and sexism in the USA today is rife with such threats.
Glenn Loury has argued, along these lines, that in recent years racial discrimination has been supplanted by ‘racial stigma’ as the key factor in the persistence of racial inequality in the United States.
Whereas discrimination involves the intentional disadvantaging of others, racial stigma is a matter of biased social cognitions and racial meanings
So while racial discrimination is no longer the problem it once was in this country, racial disadvantage continues, especially where race intersects with poverty, and it constitutes a real obstacle to freedom.
Yet the challenge it poses to freedom cannot be comprehended – or adequately addressed – so long as we continue to focus on voluntaristic forms of discrimination.

Domination through a sovereign agent

Pettit’s depiction ofdomination suggests a sovereign agent controlling a subject through the strategic use of power.
Domination involves having someone under your thumb
and coercing or intimidating him or her with a view to getting your own way.
It thus entails a dominator and a subordinate, and an awareness on both sides ofwho
holds which position.
In reality, power often operates differently.
Not only are we frequently unaware of how the power we hold affects others, but our own exercise of power in any particular instance may point in multiple directions.
Havel, for instance, presses the point that
there is no one in the system, even at the highest level, who is simply subject, dominator, sovereign agent – nor anyone who is simply object, victim, slave. Instead, ‘all are both victim and supporter of the system’.
Loury’s notion ofracial stigma suggests that Havel may be right about this.
The biased social cognitions and racial meanings that stigma entails set up a system of social interactions in which unfreedom emerges without any sovereign dominators.
For example, young black men have a difficult time getting cab drivers to pick them up on
the street because of the prevalent fear among drivers of being robbed and the presumption that young black men are more likely than others to be robbers.
Thus for most young men, anticipating a long wait will discourage dependence on taxi transportation.
But a person bent on robbery will not be so easily deterred.
As a result, there is likely to be a disproportionate number of robbers among the group of black men hailing cabs after dark.
As shown, racial stigma tends to generate self-confirming beliefs because of the patterns of social interaction it fosters.
In this case
the drivers' own behaviors have created the facts on which their pessimistic expectations are grounded.
Yet while their own behaviors contribute to the disadvantaging ofblack men
their agency in this regard is a far cry fromthe intentionality and control that Pettit’s account of domination implies.
Evidently, the obstacles to the freedom of black men that result from these complex interactions are a non-sovereign form of domination, or domination in the middle voice.
The truth is that we participate all the time in practices that subordinate us even as they perpetuate the domination ofothers, and much ofthe time these acts ofsubordination and domination are neither recognized as such nor intended,much less controlled.
Thus domination as Pettit conceives it cannot tell us all we need to know about the compromise of freedom.
Because it entails intentionality and control on the part of the dominator, it fails to capture important obstacles to freedom.
These other obstacles to freedom can be deep and disabling, and they are too often overlooked
If we let ourselves and others off the hook for violations of freedom that no one in particular intends, controls, or benefits from, we can never hope to achieve the freedom for all that liberal democracy promises – and for which liberal-democratic citizens are obligated to strive.

Agency and inequality

Pettit's focus on the capacity for intentional interference occludes the ways that informal social dynamics undercut freedom.
Pettit focuses on the fact that inequality makes one
vulnerable to the intentional exercise of arbitrary power by others.
But inequality interacts with individual agency at a deep level to impede freedom in ways that Pettit overlooks.
Krause defines 'agency' as the affirmation of one's subjective existence through concrete action in the world.
To be an agent is to affect the world; there is no agency where there are no effects.
The problem with this way of characterizing agency is that it obscures from view the ways that agency sometimes comes apart from intentionality.
our impact on the world does not always track our will
Thus we typically do not hold people responsible for influence that they did not mean to have
Yet it would be a mistake to think that unintentional actions never manifest agency.
For instance, the white employer whose decision not to hire the black candidate unintentionally reflects racial stigma is an agent of cultural racism – and a cause of the unfreedom of the black candidate – and he should be held accountable in proper measure for this.
Evidently, where a particular action falls on the agency continuum will be determined not by its degree of intentionality alone but also with reference to how fully it manifests the actor’s identity.
So agency emerges through the interaction of identity and efficacy, and the fact that our agency sometimes extends beyond our intentions makes it potent in ways that can surprise us – for better and for worse.
The scope of my agency therefore depends in part on how the subjective existence manifest in my actions is taken up by others. This uptake crucially influences the nature and extent of my effects on the world.
This inter-subjective
basis of agency
makes it clear why inequality’s effects on agency run so deep.
And it suggests that freedom, as the full flourishing of human agency, will require careful attention to social contexts of inequality, many of which are thoroughly unintentional and hence invisible from the standpoint of non-domination.

How systematic inequalities cause problems for the identity dimension of agency

If systematic inequalities cause problems for the efficacy dimension of agency, they can also be detrimental on the identity side.
Most prominently, the knowledge that one is a human being and a citizen, and therefore shares in a common identity, but is also racially distinct and therefore subject to exclusion and devaluation yields a double-consciousness.
One ‘ever feels his twoness’, Du Bois says, as ‘an
American’ and as ‘a Negro; two souls, two thoughts, two unreconciled strivings; two warring ideals in one dark body, whose dogged strength alone keeps it from being torn asunder’.
This fragmentation of the self gives rise to ‘a painful self-consciousness, an almost morbid sense of personality and a moral hesitancy which is fatal to self-confidence’
The experience of double-consciousness damages individual agency because it undercuts one’s ability to fully manifest one’s subjective existence in the world.
Instead, one second-guesses, undercuts and withholds oneself, disabling personal development and thwarting effective action.
The longing for freedom, Du Bois says, is largely a ‘longing to attain self-conscious manhood, to merge [one’s] double self into a better and truer self’.
The experience of double-consciousness and the fragmentation of the self that it entails have a chilling effect on agency.
Double-consciousness and inner fragmentation disrupt the identity dimension of agency by destabilizing the individual’s sense of her or his own subjective existence and by undercutting her or his self-respect and sense of purpose.
Thus agency can be thwarted through lack of efficacy or by factors that destabilize identity, and the dynamics of inequality affect both in ways that the domination paradigm fails to grasp.
Addressing these subtle dynamics – not just proscribing domination – will be crucial to making good on liberal democracy’s promise of freedom for all.

How freedom as non-domination overlooks cultural devaluation

As well as the case of domination in which one becomes the tool of another, unfreedom manifests itself when one's efficacy is thwarted by misrecognition, or when the identity that one manifests in the world is fragmented or stunned by the force of unconscious bigotry as a result of social inequality.
Krause argues that freedom as non-oppression
is the experience of manifesting through action one’s distinctive subjectivity fully not just partially, and in a way that is untroubled by stigma and systematic inequalities of power.
A person whose identity is deeply riven by stigma and whose efficacy is systematically undercut by it is a person in chains.
these are chains
of imprisonment, which work to confine the individual within boundaries that suffocate and disfigure.
Liberation from these bonds is an important aspect of the freedom sought by the gay liberation movement – the light- ness of being that comes from having one’s ‘inside’ and ‘outside’ lives flow together seamlessly instead of existing in perpetual opposition.
‘Coming out’ brings the freedom to manifest one’s full subjectivity in the world rather than just a fragment of it.
Free- dom in this sense also is a liberation from the double-consciousness that Du Bois described among blacks and from the stifling social norms that for far too long confined women to lives that were often ill-suited to their individual natures and aspirations
The freedom that comes with non-oppression is liberation from a cramped rather than an exploited existence, and it has long been a part of the political aspirations of margina- lized groups.

Distinguishing between oppression and domination

Oppression is frequently conflated with domination.
But oppression is an intrinsically collective phenomenon. It applies to persons only in view of their membership in particular groups.
Whereas domination requires intentionality and involves control, oppression rests on impersonal patterns of privilege.
The importance of recognition for freedom as non-oppression is better understood in terms of ‘assemblage’ and ‘acknowledgement’ models of agency than as the old politics of recognition would have it.
relevant to agency is not exactly a matter of valuing others’ identities.
Instead, it involves seeing their actions in a way that brings their self-understanding into the
picture, rather than looking at these actions exclusively through the lens of social stigma or one’s own assumptions and concerns.
Recognition so conceived is a way of looking at others but it also reminds us of the assembled character of our own agency.
Evidently, the inter-subjective basis of individual agency means that freedom as non-oppression cannot be achieved by individuals alone.
Indeed, non-oppression is a political ideal, both in the sense that it requires collective effort to achieve and in the sense that its achievement will depend on prevailing configurations of power
the power relations that affect freedom as non-oppression will include not only the formal, legally sanctioned, state-enforced power relations targeted by the non-domination ideal but also the informal social inequalities that remain outside the reach ofnon-domination because oftheir often unintentional character.
Pettit’s account of the institutional structure and civic virtues of a republican state, with its emphasis on democratic deliberation, contestation and solidarity, is valuable here.
However, achieving the ideal of non-oppresion will require more radical changes in us – democratic citizens on all sides of the inequality divide – than Pettit acknowledges.
It will involve
reconceiving the conditions of our own agency and that of others, opening our eyes to the deep dynamics of agency and inequality, and acknowledging the variety of ways beyond domination that freedom can be compromised – and the different kinds of loss that arise when it is.

Conclusions

Understanding freedom and the things that threaten it in the more capacious way
should
help us recognize and diagnose instances of unfreedom that elude the non-domination approach.
because domination is largely proscribed
in the USA and other liberal democracies today, the most persistent and pressing threats to individual freedom that we currently face come from factors other than domination, such as cultural racism and sexism.
In this sense, the approach suggested here promises to focus our attention and energies where they are likely to make the greatest difference for freedom.
Refraining from voluntary acts of domination will not be enough.
We will also want to loosen the chains of oppression that persist among us, even though this requires more than just institutional protections against arbitrary interference.
